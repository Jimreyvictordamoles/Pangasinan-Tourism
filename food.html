Pangasinan’s culture and festivals reflect the province’s deep connection to the sea, agriculture, faith, language, and tradition. Celebrations throughout the year highlight the hardworking spirit of its people — from fishermen and salt makers to farmers and artisans. Music, dance, rituals, and community gatherings play an important role in preserving identity and strengthening unity. Through these festivals and traditions, Pangasinan continues to honor its history while proudly showcasing its vibrant cultural life.

Pangasinan Food & Local Products:

Mangaldan Empanada

Quick Facts

  • Origin: Mangaldan, Pangasinan, Philippines
  • Main ingredients: Rice flour dough, grated green papaya, mung beans, egg, longganisa
  • Cooking method: Deep-fried until crisp
  • Texture: Crunchy shell with savory-soft interior
  • Occasion: Popular street and festival food

Mangaldan Empanada is a crispy fried pastry filled with longganisa (local sausage), egg, and vegetables. It’s a popular street food known for its savory and slightly sweet flavor.

Originating from the town of Mangaldan, this empanada evolved from traditional Filipino pastries but developed its own identity through the use of local Pangasinan ingredients, especially native longganisa.

It reflects Pangasinan’s love for street food and quick meals. Locals often eat it during merienda or after school/work, making it part of everyday life.

Pindang

Quick Facts

  • Origin: Pangasinan, Philippines
  • Type: Cured or dried meat
  • Main ingredients: Pork or carabao meat, vinegar, sugar, salt, spices
  • Flavor: Sweet-sour-salty, mildly smoky

Pindang is a traditional Pangasinan dish made from sun-dried or cured meat (usually carabao or beef), often marinated in vinegar, garlic, and salt. It dates back to early Pangasinense communities where preserving meat was essential due to lack of refrigeration. Drying and curing helped extend shelf life. Pindang represents resourcefulness and practicality. It’s commonly prepared during special occasions or when families store food for longer periods.

Kaleskes

Quick Facts

  • Origin: Pangasinan, Philippines
  • Main ingredients: Beef innards, skin, bones, and garlic-based broth
  • Flavor: Savory, garlicky, slightly gelatinous
  • Cultural role: Commonly sold in local eateries and roadside stalls

Kaleskes is a hearty stew made from carabao innards, bile, and spices, giving it a distinct slightly bitter taste. It originated in Dagupan and nearby towns, where locals used every part of the animal—a practice rooted in sustainability and tradition. This dish is often served during gatherings and drinking sessions, and it’s considered comfort food for many locals.

Inlubi

Quick Facts

  • Origin: Pangasinan, Philippines
  • Primary ingredient: Black glutinous rice (malagkit a itim)
  • Flavor: Coconut milk and sugar
  • Cultural role: Commonly served in Pangasinan gatherings and celebrations

Inlubi is a traditional Pangasinan dessert made from glutinous rice, coconut milk, and sugar, often topped with latik (caramelized coconut). It has roots in pre-colonial Filipino cooking, where rice and coconut were staple ingredients in making sweets. Inlubi is commonly served during festivals, family gatherings, and special occasions, symbolizing celebration and togetherness.

Pigar-Pigar

Quick Facts

  • Origin: Dagupan, Philippines
  • Main ingredients: Beef or carabao meat, onions, cabbage
  • Cooking method: Quick pan-fry (stir-fry style)
  • Cultural event: Featured at Dagupan’s annual Pigar-Pigar Festival

Pigar-Pigar is a famous street food made from thinly sliced beef, onions, and cabbage, quickly fried and served hot. It originated in Dagupan City and became popular due to its affordable and satisfying serving, especially for workers and students. It represents Pangasinan’s night food culture, often eaten in groups at roadside stalls.
